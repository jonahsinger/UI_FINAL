Budgeting Basics

Learn how to create and maintain a budget that works for you

What is a Budget?

Definition: A budget is a financial plan that tracks your income and expenses over a period of time, helping you make informed decisions about how to spend and save your money.

Think of a budget as a roadmap for your money. It shows where your money comes from, where it needs to go, and how much you can set aside for future goals. Creating a budget isn't about restricting your spendingâ€”it's about understanding your financial situation and making your money work for you.

A good budget helps you:

  • Track where your money is going
  • Identify areas where you might be overspending
  • Ensure you're saving enough for your goals
  • Reduce financial stress by providing clarity
  • Make more confident financial decisions

A budget helps you balance your income, expenses, and savings goals

The 50/30/20 Budgeting Rule

One popular and straightforward budgeting method is the 50/30/20 rule. This approach divides your after-tax income into three main categories:

50%

Needs

Essential expenses you can't avoid

  • Housing (rent/mortgage)
  • Utilities
  • Groceries
  • Transportation
  • Insurance
  • Minimum debt payments

30%

Wants

Non-essential expenses that improve your life

  • Dining out
  • Entertainment
  • Shopping
  • Hobbies
  • Subscriptions
  • Travel

20%

Savings & Debt

Building financial security

  • Emergency fund
  • Retirement accounts
  • Investments
  • Savings goals
  • Extra debt payments

This framework is a starting point that you can adjust based on your unique situation. For example, if you live in an expensive city, you might need to allocate more than 50% to needs. The key is to use this as a guideline while creating a budget that reflects your personal circumstances.

Creating Your Budget in 5 Steps

1

Calculate Your Monthly Income

Start by determining how much money you bring home each month after taxes and deductions. Include all sources of income such as:

  • Primary job salary/wages
  • Side hustle earnings
  • Rental income
  • Investment dividends

For irregular income, calculate the average monthly amount based on past earnings.

2

Track Your Expenses

List all your regular monthly expenses, separated into categories:

  • Fixed expenses: Costs that remain relatively constant each month (rent, car payment, insurance)
  • Variable expenses: Costs that fluctuate month to month (groceries, utilities, entertainment)

Review your bank and credit card statements from the past few months to identify spending patterns.

3

Set Financial Goals

Define what you're trying to achieve with your budget:

  • Short-term goals: Building an emergency fund, paying off credit card debt
  • Medium-term goals: Saving for a down payment, planning a vacation
  • Long-term goals: Retirement savings, college fund for children

Assign a specific dollar amount and timeline to each goal.

4

Allocate Your Income

Distribute your income across your expense categories and savings goals. You can use the 50/30/20 rule as a starting point:

  • 50% for needs (housing, food, utilities, etc.)
  • 30% for wants (entertainment, dining out, etc.)
  • 20% for savings and debt repayment

Adjust these percentages as needed to fit your lifestyle and goals.

5

Monitor and Adjust

A budget is not a "set it and forget it" tool. To be effective:

  • Track your actual spending regularly
  • Compare actual spending to your budgeted amounts
  • Identify areas where you're over or under budget
  • Make adjustments as needed
  • Review and update your budget when your income or expenses change

Popular Budgeting Methods

There are many approaches to budgeting. The best method is the one you'll stick with consistently. Here are some popular budgeting methods to consider:

Zero-Based Budget

Every dollar of your income is assigned a specific job (expenses, savings, debt). At the end of the month, your income minus your allocations should equal zero.

Pros: Highly detailed control, ensures all money is accounted for
Cons: Time-intensive, requires frequent adjustments

Envelope System

Cash is physically placed in labeled envelopes for different spending categories. When an envelope is empty, you stop spending in that category until the next budget period.

Pros: Tangible spending limits, helps control overspending
Cons: Less convenient for online purchases, impractical for large expenses

Pay Yourself First

Automatically direct a portion of your income to savings goals before paying any bills or discretionary spending.

Pros: Prioritizes savings, simple to implement with automatic transfers
Cons: Less detailed tracking of other spending categories

50/30/20 Budget

As discussed earlier, this method allocates 50% to needs, 30% to wants, and 20% to savings and debt repayment.

Pros: Simple to understand, provides flexibility within categories
Cons: May not work for all income levels or in all geographic locations

Key Takeaways

  • A budget is a plan for your money, not a restriction
  • The 50/30/20 rule provides a simple framework to start budgeting
  • Track all income and expenses to gain financial clarity
  • Choose a budgeting method that fits your personality and lifestyle
  • Regularly review and adjust your budget as needed
  • Budgeting is a skill that improves with practice and consistency
