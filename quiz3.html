What is the main difference between a Roth IRA and a traditional 401(k)?

A. A Roth IRA uses pre-tax contributions; a 401(k) uses after-tax contributions.
B. There is no difference.
C. A Roth IRA is funded with after-tax money, while a traditional 401(k) is usually funded with pre-tax money.
D. A 401(k) has no employer matching, while a Roth IRA always does.
Correct!

Roth IRAs typically use after-tax funds (you pay taxes now but not later), while traditional 401(k)s often use pre-tax funds (you pay taxes when you withdraw).

Incorrect

A Roth IRA is funded with after-tax money (you pay taxes now), while a traditional 401(k) is typically funded with pre-tax money (you pay taxes later when you withdraw).

Which statement is true about Roth IRAs?

A. You pay taxes on every withdrawal no matter what.
B. Qualified withdrawals in retirement are generally tax-free.
C. You never pay any taxes on contributions.
D. Roth IRAs are only available through employers.
Correct!

Since Roth IRAs are funded with after-tax dollars, qualified withdrawals during retirement are tax-free, which is one of their main benefits.

Incorrect

Roth IRAs provide tax-free qualified withdrawals in retirement. You pay taxes on the money before contributing to the account, but then you generally don't pay taxes on growth or qualified withdrawals.

Which of the following is typically a benefit of a 401(k)?

A. There is no maximum contribution limit.
B. You cannot invest in stocks within a 401(k).
C. Employer matching contributions can help your investments grow faster.
D. You must withdraw money at any time you choose without penalties.
Correct!

Many employers match a certain percentage of employee 401(k) contributions, effectively giving "free" additional funds that can significantly boost your retirement savings.

Incorrect

One of the main benefits of a 401(k) is employer matching. Many employers will match a portion of your contributions (e.g., 3-6% of your salary), which is essentially free money that can significantly boost your retirement savings over time.

At what age can you generally withdraw from your Roth IRA or 401(k) without penalty (assuming the account has existed for required timeframes)?

A. 18
B. 30
C. 59½
D. 72
Correct!

In most cases, 59½ is the age at which you can begin penalty-free withdrawals from retirement accounts, though there are some exceptions and special rules.

Incorrect

The age for penalty-free withdrawals from most retirement accounts is 59½. Withdrawals before this age generally incur a 10% penalty (though there are some exceptions).

True or False: You should consider both tax treatment now (contributions) and in the future (withdrawals) when choosing between a Roth IRA and a traditional 401(k).

A. True
B. False
Correct!

Understanding both your current tax bracket and your expected retirement tax bracket will help you decide which account type is most beneficial. If you expect higher taxes in retirement, a Roth may be better; if you expect lower taxes in retirement, a traditional 401(k) may be better.

Incorrect

It's important to consider both current and future tax implications when choosing retirement accounts. Your tax rate now versus your expected tax rate in retirement should influence whether you choose a Roth IRA (pay taxes now) or traditional 401(k) (pay taxes later).
