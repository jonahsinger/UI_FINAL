Compounding returns means:

A. Earning returns on your principal only.
B. Earning returns on both your principal and any accumulated returns.
C. Paying more taxes each year.
D. None of the above.
Correct!

With compounding, your earnings are reinvested, so each new round of returns is calculated on a larger base that includes both your original investment and previously earned returns.

Incorrect

Compounding returns means earning returns on both your principal and any accumulated returns. This creates a snowball effect where growth accelerates over time.

Which of these formulas best represents compound interest?

A. A = P + rt
B. A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)
C. A = P(1 - r)^(t)
D. None of the above.
Correct!

Compound interest is calculated using the principal multiplied by one plus the interest rate divided by compounding periods, raised to the total compounding periods over time.

Incorrect

The compound interest formula is A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt), where A is the final amount, P is the principal, r is the interest rate, n is compounding frequency, and t is time in years.

If you start investing earlier, compounding returns are typically:

A. Less beneficial because you have more time to lose money.
B. Unchanged by the time factor.
C. More beneficial because your money compounds for a longer period.
D. Irrelevant compared to making a lump sum investment at the end.
Correct!

The longer your money is invested, the more time it has to earn returns on top of returns, accelerating growth. This is why starting early is one of the most powerful investing strategies.

Incorrect

Starting to invest earlier is significantly more beneficial because it gives your money more time to compound. Even small amounts invested early can outperform larger amounts invested later due to the power of compound returns over time.

A key difference between simple interest and compound interest is:

A. Simple interest pays on principal plus accumulated returns, while compound interest only pays on principal.
B. Simple interest pays only on principal, while compound interest pays on principal + prior returns.
C. Simple interest grows faster.
D. There is no difference.
Correct!

Compound interest is more powerful because it reinvests returns, leading to exponential growth over time, unlike simple interest which only calculates returns on the original principal.

Incorrect

With simple interest, you only earn returns on your original principal. With compound interest, you earn returns on both your principal and previously earned returns, which creates accelerating growth over time.

True or False: The compounding frequency (monthly vs. annually) has no effect on the final amount.

A. True
B. False
Correct!

More frequent compounding (e.g., monthly vs. annually) increases the rate at which returns are reinvested, resulting in a higher final amount over time.

Incorrect

Compounding frequency does affect the final amount. More frequent compounding (monthly, daily, etc.) results in higher returns compared to less frequent compounding (annually) because returns are calculated and reinvested more often.
